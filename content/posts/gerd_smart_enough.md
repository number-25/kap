title: "Are we smart enough, Gerd?"
date: 2025-10-15T04:21:12Z

A friend of mine once remarked that "We are not smart enough for our age
[epoch]". What could they have meant by this? Education levels across the
world are remarkably high, and many believe ourselves capable of good
decision making. Yet, many aspects of the world around us elude, deceive, and
confuse us. From time to time we find that it is exactly those who should know, who are
falling short of expectations. In these moments, that glossy shine of modernity, brittles away so slightly, and we feel fooled. 

No doubt, there is naturally opposition to this broad statement --- one would say
that, *No! we are in fact smart enough for our age, we have mastery over the
skies and the earth, our advancement in technology is not comparable to any
epoch before us*. It is true, surely, us, taken as a whole, are collectively
smart enough; and who could be surprised, as much of this world has been
established by us. Indeed, we do know how electricity works, we do know about
carbon dating, we can perform heart surgery. And yet, why do surveys find that most doctors do not
know how to weigh the benefits and dangers of simple medical screens, this ought to be their expertise? To
resolve this butting of heads, the *we* here refers to sum of individuals
navigating life as independent beings, as opposed to the totality of the human species and all of its
information.  We can simultaneously know how to build atomic weaponry, whilst
having the majority of our world be statistically illiterate. The two are mostly
unrelated. On one hand the repository of all human knowledge, the other, the
understanding embodied in a single person. 

Maybe, "we as people, are not smart enough for this age". The social
scientist Gerd Gigerenzer might say, "We as people, are not *risk literate*
enough for this age". The way I take it then, is that my friend was discussing
us as people encountering world, living our lives in modern society, with all
it's technological features. Gerd, in his book *Risk Savvy*, opens by touching
on something similar;

> Literacy---the ability to read and write---is the lifeblood of an informed citizenship in a democracy. But knowing how to read and write isn't enough. *Risk literacy* is the basic knowledge required to deal with a modern technology society. The breakneck speed of technological innovation will make risk literacy as indespensable in the twenty-first century as reading and writing were in previous centuries. Without it, you jeopardize your health and money, or may be manipulated into unrealistic fears and hopes. One might think that the basics of risk literacy are already being taught. Yet you will look in vain for it in most high schools, law schools, medical school, and beyond. As a result, most of us are risk illiterate.

I feel that this is what we're getting at here. Not to pile upon our inadequacies---but put in another way---
we are not adjusted, adapted enough to our age. We are still playing catchup, running, trying to keep
up with the pace of it all, the speed and volume of information, and the
seeming legitimacy that it holds. Our tools, techniques and methods to navigate
and interpret aspects of our time, are simply not developed sufficiently. 

As moderns, I don't think it misguided to say that we excel in putting forth
predictions, and specifically, quantifying those predictions. Through our
technologies of measurement, and our analytical methods, presenting
probabilities of events are a regular, if not, default characteristic of our
communication. Perhaps we could go so far as say they are an expectation at
this stage. At our doctors offices we seek likelihoods of *x*, we hear
weather-women telling us of rains and droughts, financial analysts the trends in
exchange rates for next year. We are accustomed to think numerically and so the
allegations of illiteracy do strike us unjustified. Despite this numeracy, how often do we
take the next step---putting confidence values on those
predictions themselves---and---placing them under scrutiny. They are taken for
granted, as concrete as heat from fire. Not all numbers are created equally, unfortunately. 

Looking at some examples, Gerd describes an interactive lecture that
he presented to a room of gynecologists at a medical networking event. In
Australia, the time it takes to receive a qualification to practice gynecology is 10
years at minimum, with 12-14 years being more common. These are
highly qualified individuals, perhaps the most so in our societies. He presented the room with a question; *"If a
women undergoes a routine mammography screening, and tests positive, what is
the likelihood that she actually has breast cancer?"*. He proceeded to provide
the rates of breast cancer among women, and the test efficacy statistics; rates of true to false
positives and negatives. Collecting the doctors answers via an interactive
polling system, he found that only 21 percent of the doctors would correctly
inform women of their likelihood of having breast cancer given a positive
screening. One has to wonder what could be going wrong here. Much of Gerd's
work is dedicated to understanding why these experts are not as literate as
they should be, and fortunately, many of his answers provide redemption. These
same test efficacy statistics, when transformed into natural frequencies, often
allow people to solve these same problems which were once too complicated when
presented in a classical probabilistic form[^1][^2][^3]. This could be as simple as
transforming a percentage to an easily parsed ratio e.g. 1% to 1 in 100, and performing a rudimentary calculation on the input values, allowing the answer to reveal itself far more obviously.   

Even then, leaving it in doctors hands doesn't sit well, personally. Our technological
sophistication has led to high degrees of domain specific knowledge which are
acquired through these years of education. It does feel as though everybody is
represented as an expert at something. Some of these disciplines are defined to
a degree by their mandate to make these forecasts. A sort of monopoly of
legitimate prediction, ala, the state and it's violence. They are provided such
social mandates because they are experts (note the dangers of this cyclicality if the quality of experts is compromised). As such, it is entirely normal to put
our trust in them, and a trust that is held in high regard is a pillar for
social cohesion. There is a really sinister side of this illiteracy---there are
real people who bear the consequences of misdiagnosis---bodies are mutilated,
made incontinent, subjected to hopeless chemotherapeutic treatments with
troubling side effects, tremendous resources wasted along the way. These are
all features of a technological age which should know better, which will know
better. Gerd's insistence is that we should no longer view this as a hole that even more
domain knowledge is needed to fill, but general, broad sweeping risk and statistical
literacy from the base up. I was shocked to read that sixth graders' performance with natural frequencies matched the performance of adults with probabilities graders[^4]. The organisation of information matters, not just it's existence!

These shortcomings become even more urgent, and are compounded by the
intentional misrepresentation of information; naked deception. Across numerous
fields, not just health and medicine, there are bodies who purposefully
misconstrue for their own gain. A conscious propagation of misunderstanding,
most often for monetary benefit and gain. Opening up a webpage, our University
is boasting of a research group that has found a 20% increase in survival,
which, standing on it's own, seems remarkable. That is until the absolute
increase is revealed. Survival has increased from 1 in 10'000 to 1.2 in 10'000.
Would you get on a treatment for a disease which increased your survival
chances from 1 to 1.2 in 10'000? To hammer a previous point, let us not forget
the dark flip side of these interventions, the side effects. Thinking both
ways, in both directions, is a feature of risk literacy. 

Living in freezing environments necessitates knowledge about how to heat
ourselves and our homes, when it is safe to move about, how we can feed
ourselves. Surely we don't want to just survive, but thrive too, and live good
lives. While our technology rich societies may not pose immediate dangers to
our lives the way the deep cold does, it is not so different here. Do not
forgot, just as there are those who die in the freezing wilderness for lack of
preparation and understanding of the climate, there are those who have also
perished due to their lack of understanding of this dimension of our new
environments. Unfortunately, it is often those who bring us into the blizzard,
that are convinced we are going to oasis. And so, living in an environment, a
world that is flooding, and steeped in information represented in statistical
and numerical form, it becomes a necessity to acquire an understanding of how
to live well in this world; and just as we ignore the chill of the cold on a
freezing night, when we ignore probabilities and risk literacy, it is as our own peril. How often do those who are triggered by online rage bait, reflect on whether they are
being hoodwinked? Trapped in an artificial, algorithmically generated pocket of
reality where the entire purpose is to present the events in question as being
more important than they likely are, to amplify the probabilities of their
actual existence. One cannot fault us entirely for our misreadings, it is not often in reality that we see an object or environment, and have to question what the likelihood that this environment *actually* exists is --- the object and environment are given, we see, smell, touch them. 

A second type of response that's often put forward to this notion that 'we
are not smart enough for our age' goes something like "*No, we are smart
enough, we have the tools, and this is exactly how you solve it, this is the
optimal solution to the problem*." While the first response defers to
expertise, which, paradoxically, doesn't exclude experts themselves who
actually defer to their own believed expertise, the second response often
assumes the position of the expert themselves, and defers to specific
techniques and technologies. According to Gerd, two common approaches are
encountered; a) newer is better, and b) optimisation with more information. In
the 'newer is better' approach, the solution to a problem is sought by finding
the most sophisticated and thus typically newest method of answering it. 

An example; acute vestibular syndrome typically manifests itself with dizziness,
nausea and vomiting. When a patient enters the ER, doctors need to *quickly*
rule out whether the patient has had a stroke, which in rare cases causes this
syndrome. The doctors can either send the patient for an MRI, which takes 15-90
minutes and costs over $1000, or, as skilled doctors had done for decades,
undertake a bedside exam known as HINTS. HINTS is a quick manual evaluation
where eye tracking and visual responsiveness is used as a reliable proxy for
whether the patient has this syndrome. In a study of 101 high-risk patients
with acute vestibular syndrome, diagnosis was mode with both an MRI and with
the HINTS exam. While MRI missed strokes in 8 out of 76 patients, the HINTS
examination caught all correctly and did not miss one. The exam caused one
false alarm amongst the patients without stroke. In total, the HINTS exam was
far quicker, far cheaper, far more comfortable for the patients, and most
importantly, it was in fact more accurate than the multimillion dollar MRI
device. To bang the drum once more, this is one of Gerd's most committed
points; there are methods which are fast, frugal, relying on minimal
information, yet, when compared to shinier methods, actually outperform them.  

Being risk literate involves knowing when to use certain techniques and tools over others, and not being seduced by the latest developments for the sake of novelty. In a climate of rapid change and acceleration, this can be a trying demand.  

Optimisation with increased information is a roundabout way of saying that we add more
data into an equation, or set of equations, and compute the optimal solution to
said problem. These types of problems are often found within the realms of
economics and engineering, but there are notable other examples. In *Risk Savvy*,
Gerd describes a common scenario for those looking to invest some of their
money in a diverse way and to avoid putting all their eggs in one basket. A common goal here is to minimize risk for a given return on ones
investment. Harry Markowitz won a Novel Prize in economics for solving this
problem, demonstrating that there is an *optimal* way to allocate ones funds
--- a mathematically correct solution. This and similar methods are employed by banking and financial firms for their investments. Where the story gets
peculiar, is that when asked about his investment retirement strategy,
Markowitz decided to use a simple rule of thumb, the 1/N method. Split your
investments equally. Love all your children the same. When asked why he didn't
use his Nobel method, he replied by saying he didn't want to be embarrassed if
the stock market acted differently than to what he expected! Why the caution
from the one who outlined an optimal strategy? When 1/N was compared against
Markowitz's own, and a dozen other complex methods in seven real world
situations, such as picking ten American industry funds, 1/N outperformed all
of the methods in 6 of the 7 situations, as judged by common performance
criteria[^7].  

A lastly notable example from the book; we go to a restaurant for the first
time, without any background information about it. What do we order? Is there
an optimal answer to this problem, one where we will maximize our
satisfaction and find the *best* item on the menu for us? Let's go pursuing
this optimal solution, off we go, creating a decision matrix from all the
items, assigning scores to each item, then calculating a maximum pleasure
score for the one we should choose. Or... should we order all the items and
taste eat one slightly? Well, perhaps now, in the realm of LLMs, perhaps such
data compression is possible, so we pull our phone out and ask our chatbot. I
hope it's not surprising to say that all these methods are frankly a bit
ludicrous, no? Even pulling out our phone and trawling the chat engine has a
smell of the pathetic to it. The quickest, and likely most effective solution,
is just asking the waiter what the crowd favorites are, and choosing amongst
these. Assuming we're at a respectable establishment that isn't trying to sell
you their almost-off steaks, you're likely to be happy. I like this example as
it magnifies certain core restrictions, and limits on our everyday life --- time is
not infinite, computing power is not infinite, wealth is not infinite, we cannot sample infinitely.
Effective decision making is both accurate, that is, get's close to the correct
answer, and is also aligned to the real, living situation at hand. There is far
more than simply correctness or optimality, but speed, repeatability, clarity, simplicity --- the chase for optimality may thus obscure actual decision making.
Perhaps with all the dishes at hand, and a year to waste, we could find what we
best enjoy, but then on the first of January, a new head chef would arrive, and
we'd be back to square one. 

This quest for optimality, and this bias for optimisation as a default problem
solving method, has additional pertinence in the present moment, where AI hype
is ballooning expectations on many facets of our lives. It is often believed
that answers to a problem are all found at the end of a very serious
calculation, with shortcomings stemming from inadequate data, rather than
misguided method. Here we have manifested an insatiable appetite: more
compute, more parameters, more data, more electricity. One would be led to the
thought that human mind was a 200sq metre data center. Let's not charter into the worlds where optimal solutions may not be computable at all. A moments reflection follows thereafter. 

Gerd, in *Risk Savvy* has distilled the importance of understanding ones
domain, and viewing it in much the same way as an environment straight
out of "nature" itself, with qualities and attributes which ought to be
taken as seriously as a strong gust of wind. Thanks to the chasm of time passed
by our ancestors, we have adaptive strategies which extremely effectively solve
natural problems, and well, time moves forwards, and we need new strategies to add to our adaptive toolkits, for today and tomorrow. We have remarkably powerful tools and techniques, *but*, we need the literacy to know their limits, and the honesty to confront hubris and overconfidence.

Gerd might say, we are NOT smart enough, but we can be savvy enough!

*No doubt this has implications for democracy, how important it is to wrestle
the power from those who believe us best managed by small groups of elites -
the information from a group can approach the correct answer better (Galton pig
weighing) - the elitist Galton. - the topic of another post*
